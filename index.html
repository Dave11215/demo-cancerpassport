Understanding Clinical Trials

How participating in research can benefit you and future patients

Making an informed decision: Clinical trials are a critical part of cancer research, leading to new treatments and improved care. This guide will help you understand the potential benefits, protections, and what to expect if you choose to participate.

Every cancer treatment available today exists because patients like you participated in clinical trials. These studies test new ways to prevent, detect, or treat cancer, helping doctors understand what works best.

Active Role in Care

üõ°Ô∏è Your Rights and Protections

Federal regulations protect clinical trial participants:

  • Informed Consent: You must receive clear information about the study, including potential risks and benefits, before deciding to participate6
  • IRB Oversight: An Institutional Review Board reviews and monitors all studies to protect participant safety and rights7
  • Voluntary Participation: You can leave a clinical trial at any time, for any reason, without affecting your regular care8
  • Privacy Protection: Your personal health information is protected under HIPAA and other regulations9
  • Ongoing Monitoring: Studies are continuously monitored for safety, and can be stopped if concerns arise10

‚ùì Common Questions and Concerns

Will I receive a placebo instead of treatment?

‚ùå Common Misconception

"I'll just get a sugar pill and no real treatment"

‚úì The Reality

In cancer trials, placebos are rarely used alone. Most studies compare new treatments to the current standard of care, so you'll receive at least the standard treatment.11

What about costs?

The study drug and research-related procedures are typically provided at no cost. Your insurance usually covers routine care costs, just as they would outside of a trial. Many centers have financial counselors to help you understand any potential costs.12

How much time will it take?

Time commitments vary by study. Some require only a few extra visits, while others involve more frequent monitoring. Your study team will clearly explain the schedule before you decide to participate.13

What if the treatment doesn't work for me?

Your care team will monitor you closely throughout the trial. If the treatment isn't working or causes unacceptable side effects, you can stop participating and return to standard care at any time.14

Clinical trial participation has led to major breakthroughs:

Breast Cancer

Clinical trials led to targeted therapies like Herceptin, improving survival rates for HER2-positive breast cancer by 33%.15

Immunotherapy

Checkpoint inhibitors discovered through trials now help patients with melanoma, lung cancer, and many other types achieve long-term remission.16

üß¨

Precision Medicine

Trials testing targeted therapies based on genetic profiles have led to personalized treatments with fewer side effects.17

‚è±Ô∏è

Quality of Life

Studies focusing on supportive care have improved symptom management and quality of life during treatment.18

üåç Addressing Health Disparities

Your participation is especially valuable if you're from an underrepresented community. Historically, clinical trials haven't included diverse populations, which means treatments may not work equally well for everyone.19

When trials include people of different ages, races, ethnicities, and backgrounds, researchers can better understand how treatments work for all patients. Your participation helps ensure future treatments are safe and effective for people like you.20

üó£Ô∏è Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • 1. What is the purpose of this study?
  • 2. What are the possible benefits and risks for me?
  • 3. How does this compare to my standard treatment options?
  • 4. What tests and procedures are involved?
  • 5. How long will the study last?
  • 6. What will my time commitment be?
  • 7. Will I have any costs? What will insurance cover?
  • 8. Who will be in charge of my care?
  • 9. Can I continue seeing my regular doctor?
  • 10. What happens if I want to stop participating?

Your Decision, Your Timeline

Deciding whether to join a clinical trial is personal. Take time to learn about your options, discuss them with your loved ones and healthcare team, and make the choice that's right for you. There's no pressure to decide immediately.
