Clinical Trials 101

How research becomes treatment

Clinical trials are carefully designed studies that test new ways to prevent, detect, or treat cancer. Each phase builds on what was learned before — from safety (Phase I) to effectiveness (Phase III). Taking part helps doctors improve future care, and participation is always voluntary.

What is a clinical trial?

It’s a research study that tests new ways to prevent, find, or treat cancer. Trials follow a written plan (a protocol), are reviewed by ethics boards, and include careful safety monitoring.

Trial phases

Phase I studies test safety and dose. Phase II looks for early signs of effectiveness and side effects. Phase III compares the new approach to the current standard to confirm benefit and risk.

Your rights & safety

You’ll receive an informed consent form explaining the purpose, procedures, risks, benefits, and alternatives. Asking questions is encouraged, and you can stop at any time without affecting your usual care.

Potential benefits

  • Access to new approaches not yet widely available
  • Careful monitoring by a research team
  • Helping improve future treatments for others

Potential risks

  • Unknown or unexpected side effects
  • New treatment may not work better than standard care
  • Extra visits, tests, or time commitments

Common questions

How do I find trials that may fit me?

Talk with your oncology team and explore NCI’s trial search or ClinicalTrials.gov. They list eligibility criteria and contacts.

Will I get a placebo?

In cancer trials, placebos are rarely used alone. If a placebo is part of the design, it’s typically added to standard therapy—never in place of appropriate care.

Does joining a trial affect my regular care?

Your usual care team remains involved. Trials are designed to complement standard care, and you can withdraw if it’s not right for you.

What does “randomized” mean?

Participants are assigned by chance to one of the study groups to make fair comparisons. Randomization helps researchers learn which approach works best.
